# Chapter 3: Steel, Coal, and the Long Decline

> *"The mines closed. The men who worked them did not disappear. They are still here, in the cafés, on the trams, in the voting booths. They remember. Their children remember. The city remembers."*
> — from the orientation notes of Dieter Kranz, Sheriff of Essen

---

*You will hunt among these people. They are not your herd yet — not in the sense that matters. They are a population with a history, and that history shapes their movements, their fears, their allegiances, and their blood. I have prepared this brief on the mortal city because I have seen too many Kindred arrive in Essen believing they understand Germans and discovering they understand nothing about the Ruhr.*

*The Ruhr is not Germany. It is something older, something shaped by coal and steel and the long, slow work of surviving the end of both. The people who live here are descended from miners and foundry workers who were promised that their labour would be remembered. It was not. The institutions that replaced the mines did not replace the dignity. The city is still waiting.*

*Learn this history. It is under everything else.*

— K.W.

## The Ruhr Before Steel (pre-1811)

Before coal, the Ruhr was agricultural — villages, monasteries, the slow rhythms of the Holy Roman Empire. Essen itself grew around a convent founded in the ninth century, the Stift Essen, which gave the city its name and its earliest institutional identity: a place governed by abbesses who administered land, collected tithes, and maintained order for eight hundred years. The abbey was dissolved in 1803 as part of Napoleon's reorganization of the German ecclesiastical states. The abbess's authority ended. The habit of institutional governance did not.

This matters to Kindred because it explains something about Essen that outsiders consistently misunderstand. The city has been governed by institutions longer than it has been governed by individuals. When Friedrich von Hügel arrived in 1858, he did not need to teach Essen to accept institutional authority. The city already knew how. He simply inserted himself into the machinery.

---

## The Krupp Century (1811–1914)

In 1811, Friedrich Krupp founded a cast-steel works in Essen. Within fifty years, Krupp was the largest industrial enterprise in Europe, employing tens of thousands, arming the Prussian army, and reshaping the Ruhr into a landscape of blast furnaces, colliery towers, and company housing. The Krupp firm did not merely operate in Essen. It was Essen. It built the workers' housing, ran the company stores, maintained the hospitals, administered the pensions, and, for all practical purposes, governed the city.

The Krupp family lived at Villa Hügel, a 269-room mansion completed in 1873 on a hill overlooking the Baldeneysee. The mansion was a statement: the Krupps did not live among their workers. They observed them from above. The architecture announced that the distance between the family and the city was not merely economic but physical, visible, permanent.

For Kindred, the Krupp century provided a feeding environment of extraordinary stability. A population concentrated in company housing, working predictable shifts, moving through the city along fixed routes. The domain's early feeding territories were built around the Krupp workforce — not because Friedrich von Hügel controlled Krupp directly, but because he understood industrial logistics better than any mortal manager, and he applied that understanding to the distribution of hunting grounds with the same precision he brought to everything else.

The Krupp empire peaked in 1914. The First World War, which Krupp helped arm, would begin its long dissolution.

---

## War, Collapse, and Reconstruction (1914–1960)

The First World War broke Krupp's monopoly on German armaments. The Treaty of Versailles, the occupation of the Ruhr by French and Belgian forces in 1923, and the hyperinflation of the Weimar years dismantled the company's economic dominance. Workers who had never known anything but Krupp found themselves unemployed, radicalized, and increasingly receptive to the promises of both Communists and National Socialists.

The Second World War did to Essen what it did to every German industrial city: bombs. The Krupp works were a priority target for Allied air raids. By 1945, ninety percent of central Essen was destroyed. The survivors lived in cellars, then in rubble, then in the hastily constructed housing of the *Wirtschaftswunder*. The reconstruction was rapid, pragmatic, and largely indifferent to aesthetics. Essen rebuilt itself as a functioning city, not a beautiful one.

The post-war settlement had a particular character in Essen. The Krupp family's remaining assets were placed in a foundation — the Alfried Krupp von Bohlen und Halbach-Stiftung — whose charter specified that it would support culture, science, and education, and whose board included municipal appointees. The company that had ruled Essen for a century became a philanthropic institution. The workers who had served it became citizens of a city that no longer had an identity beyond having survived.

This is the Essen that most Kindred find when they arrive: a city that was once something and is no longer sure what it is now.

---

## The *Strukturwandel* (1960–2000)

*Strukturwandel* — structural change — is the German word for what happened to the Ruhr in the second half of the twentieth century. The coal mines closed. The steel mills contracted. The workforce that had built the German economic miracle was told that its skills were no longer needed and that the future lay in services, technology, culture. The Ruhr would become a "knowledge region." The miners would become something else.

They did not. Not all of them. The transition from industrial to post-industrial employment was partial, painful, and uneven. By the 1990s, Essen's unemployment rate was among the highest in western Germany. The city had universities, theaters, museums, a Philharmonie ranked among the world's finest, and a population that still voted for the Social Democratic Party out of loyalty to a working-class identity that no longer corresponded to working-class employment.

The *Strukturwandel* is not over. It has been underway for sixty years and shows no sign of completion. Essen is a city in transition from one thing to another thing, and the destination keeps changing. This makes it unstable in ways that are useful to Kindred and dangerous to everyone.

## Essen Today: The City Your Herd Lives In (2000–Present)

The Essen of the present is a city of 580,000 mortals, the ninth-largest in Germany, the cultural anchor of the Ruhr. It is not wealthy by German standards, but it is not poor. It is not growing, but it is not shrinking. It is not famous, and it prefers it that way. Ask a German about Essen and they will mention something about coal, or Krupp, or the A40 autobahn closing for a cultural festival. They will not mention the city's soul. They do not know it has one.

The city's mortal institutions are stable, underfunded, and deeply entangled with one another. The municipal administration is competent but under-resourced. The cultural sector — the Aalto-Theater, the Philharmonie, Museum Folkwang, the Grillo-Theater — operates at a level of quality that astonishes visitors and goes largely unremarked upon by residents. The university quarter near the Stadtgarten attracts students from across North Rhine-Westphalia, providing a feeding demographic that renews itself every four years without the domain having to do anything to facilitate it.

The city's geography is shaped by its industrial past. The Ruhr River curves through the south. The Baldeneysee, a reservoir created in 1933, stretches along the southern edge. The northern districts — Altenessen, Katernberg, Stoppenberg — are working-class, immigrant-heavy, still marked by the mine closures. The southern districts — Bredeney, Werden, the Villa Hügel grounds — are green, quiet, and wealthy in ways that feel almost embarrassed. The center is a pedestrian zone, rebuilt after the war, functional rather than evocative.

The A40 autobahn cuts the city in half, east to west. Germans call it the *Ruhrschleichweg* — the Ruhr crawlway — because it is perpetually congested. Kindred call it the border. North of the A40 is the Sheriff's territory. South is the Keeper's. Both would deny that this division exists.

## What This Means for You

You will hunt here. You will feed from people whose grandparents mined coal and whose parents lost their jobs in the *Strukturwandel* and who themselves work in call centers, university administration offices, or the cultural institutions that replaced the factories. They will taste, I am told, of resilience and resignation in roughly equal measure — a flavour profile that older Kindred in this domain consider an acquired taste and that I have never acquired.

They are not prey. They are a population that has survived the end of its world twice in living memory and is preparing to do so a third time. They have institutions that protect them imperfectly but persistently. They have a local identity that outsiders cannot parse and that they cannot explain. They will sense that you do not belong to them long before they understand what you are, and they will not confront you about it. They will simply stop telling you things.

The Sheriff has more detailed notes on hunting practice in the sealed sections of this dossier. Read them when they are opened to you. Until then, observe the feeding restrictions, register your territory, and do not underestimate the mortals of the Ruhr.

— K.W.
