# Chapter 14: Dieter Kranz and Eva Sonnenberg

> *"The Sheriff believes the domain is held together by loyalty. The Keeper believes it is held together by beauty. They are both wrong. It is held together by the fact that neither of them has ever been wrong about anything important at the same time."*
> — Dr. Katharina Weiss, private journal, 2018

---

*The Sheriff and the Keeper do not like each other. This is well known in the domain, and it is wrong. They like each other intensely. They disagree about almost everything, and the disagreement is the only relationship either of them has ever maintained across decades without becoming bored.*

*I asked each of them to write their own dossier entry. The Sheriff's is first. The Keeper's follows. I have not edited them. The differences in register, emphasis, and opinion are the point.*

— K.W.

---

## DIETER KRANZ, SHERIFF OF ESSEN

*The Seneschal asked me to write this. She said to be honest. I told her I don't know how to be anything else. I've been Sheriff since 1988. Before that I was a union man. Before that I was a foundry worker. None of these things are as different as the Kindred who only know one of them seem to think.*

### Who I Was

I was Embraced in 1962. I was thirty-seven. I had been working at the Krupp foundry since I was fourteen — the war ended and there I was, a boy with no schooling and a body that could pour steel, and that was my life for twenty-three years. I organized. That's what you need to know about me. I was a shop steward for IG Metall before my Embrace. I spent my mortal life arguing with management about wages and conditions and the dignity of men who had nothing to sell but their labour.

My sire was a Brujah named Lukas Ebert. He was old, older than Friedrich, older than anyone I've met since. He Embraced me because he was dying — not from violence, just from time, the way Kindred die when they've been alive too long and can't remember why. He needed someone who would stay in Essen, who would watch the northern districts, who would understand that the mortals in this city are not cattle. He found a union man who had spent his life understanding exactly that.

Ebert died in 1967. I buried him in a shaft near Katernberg. I'm the only Kindred who knows where. That's the way he wanted it.

### What I Do

I'm the Sheriff. That means I enforce the Masquerade, investigate breaches, maintain domain security. In practice, it means I know every northern district's alleys and basements and transit corridors well enough to track a Kindred by the pattern of their feeding. It means I have two deputies — Karl and Marie, both Brujah, both Embraced in the nineties, both loyal to me in ways I don't always deserve. It means I spend most of my nights in Altenessen and Katernberg, where the mines closed and the people stayed, and I know those people — not as vessels, as people — and I will not let them be hunted like prey.

I'm sixty-two years dead. I still vote SPD. You can laugh. I don't care.

### What I Know

I know that the domain's feeding ecology is a political map disguised as a resource map. I know that the Nosferatu don't report their numbers because they don't want to be counted. I know that the Anarchs are right about the city declining and wrong about whose fault it is. I know that Friedrich has been withdrawing for decades and that nobody wants to be the one who finally says it out loud.

In 1998, I followed a Nosferatu warden into a tunnel I wasn't supposed to know about. I got maybe two hundred meters before the tunnel changed — the walls became something that wasn't concrete, the air became something that wasn't air, and I heard something breathing that wasn't on the other side of the wall. It was in the wall. I turned back. I've never told anyone what I heard. I'm telling you now because the Seneschal said to be honest, and because if you're going to be Sheriff of this domain someday, or if you're going to hunt in the districts I protect, you need to know that there are tunnels in this city that are not on any map I'm authorized to share, and something in those tunnels is breathing, and it's been breathing for longer than Essen has had a name.

Keep your deputies close. Don't send them into the old mines alone. Listen to the Nosferatu when they tell you to turn back. I didn't. I'm still here. That doesn't mean I should be.

— D.K.

## EVA SONNENBERG, KEEPER OF ELYSIUM

*The Sheriff has written about the streets. I write about the spaces the streets lead to. In my experience, the streets are where Kindred go to forget that they were once capable of beauty. Elysium is where they remember.*

### Who I Was

I was Embraced in 1924. I was thirty-one. I was a set designer at the Grillo-Theater — not the leading designer, never that, but competent, precise, someone who understood that the audience does not see the set but feels it. My sire was a Toreador architect named Wilhelm Gerlach, who designed Essen's post-war reconstruction and who Embraced me because he was dying and because I was the only mortal he had ever met who could describe a room the way he experienced it — as emotion made spatial.

I have been Keeper of Elysium since 1975, when the previous Keeper — a Toreador named Margarete Koch, Embraced in 1898 — was destroyed by a mortal hunter who had been tracking her for six years and whom none of us had noticed. The lesson I drew from this was that beauty is not protection. Visibility is not safety. The spaces that feel most welcoming are the spaces where Kindred are most likely to forget that they are being watched.

### What I Do

I maintain the domain's three Elysia — the Ghost Station, the Grillo-Theater, and Museum Folkwang. Maintenance is not custodial. It is political.

The Ghost Station is the largest neutral space in any Ruhr domain. I have spent forty years curating it — the lighting, the acoustics, the seating arrangements, the sightlines, the particular resonance of a space that was built for crowds and instead holds silences. A Kindred who enters the Ghost Station enters a room that has been designed to make them feel observed without being threatened. The architecture is intentional. The effect is control.

The Grillo-Theater is my most difficult territory. It is an active mortal theater; Elysium here is conditional on performance nights and theatrical conduct. I have expelled seventeen Kindred from the Grillo since 1975. The expulsions have been challenged twice. I prevailed both times. The domain has learned that arguing with me about theater etiquette is an argument the domain will lose.

Museum Folkwang is the domain's diplomatic space. The collection — nineteenth-century European art, Expressionist works hidden during the Nazi period, postwar acquisitions of taste and restraint — is the most cultured argument Essen can make for its continued relevance. I have brought every visiting Kindred of significance to the Folkwang since 1980. None have been unmoved. The ones who were unmoved were Kindred I advised the Seneschal not to acknowledge.

### What I Know

I know that the Sheriff is wrong about the domain being held together by loyalty. Loyalty is personal and finite and dies with the people who feel it. Beauty is institutional. A space that has been curated to feel welcoming will continue to feel welcoming long after the curator is gone. The Ghost Station will be neutral ground when I am dust. That is the point.

I know that Essen is not a beautiful city in the conventional sense — Düsseldorf took that role, and Düsseldorf is welcome to it. Essen's beauty is structural. It is the beauty of a city that survived its own destruction and rebuilt itself without pretending the destruction never happened. The Philharmonie was built on a parking lot. The Folkwang was rebuilt from rubble. The Grillo survived a war and two fires. These are not elegant histories. They are honest ones.

I know that the Sheriff heard something in the tunnel in 1998. He has never discussed it with me, but I have noticed that he has not entered the underground alone since. I have not asked. I consider it one of the few things Dieter Kranz is afraid of, and I do not wish to know what it is.

I know that the Opera Phenomenon — the music that plays in the underground without a source, in real time with no acoustic delay — is not a phenomenon. It is a signal. I have been standing in the Philharmonie lobby during a performance, sixty meters above the tunnel where the music is heard, and I have felt the vibration through the floor. Something is listening to our music. Something is answering. I do not know what. I know that whatever it is, it has been listening for longer than the city has been making sound.

— E.S.

---

*I do not know what the Sheriff and the Keeper discuss when they meet alone. I know that they meet — perhaps twice a year, in the Ghost Station, after hours. I know that neither records these meetings, and neither has ever told me what is said. I suspect they talk about the thing in the tunnel. I suspect Dieter tells Eva what he heard, and Eva tells Dieter what she senses, and neither of them tells me because telling me would oblige me to act on what they know.*

*I am the domain's memory. I remember things so the domain can forget them. The Sheriff and the Keeper are the domain's conscience. They remember things so the domain can survive them. The distinction matters. Do not ask me to explain it further.*

— K.W.
