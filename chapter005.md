# Chapter 5: Living Essen: The City Today

> *"The city looks like it lost something and decided to keep going anyway. This is not an aesthetic. It is a survival strategy."*
> — Eva Sonnenberg, Keeper of Elysium, in conversation with a visiting Toreador, 2017

---

*This chapter describes the mortal city as you will experience it. I have tried to be specific about neighborhoods, transport, and the feeding environment because I have seen too many Kindred arrive with a mental map of Berlin or Cologne and spend their first month in Essen disoriented and hungry. The city does not announce itself. You have to learn to read it.*

— K.W.

## The Shape of the City

Essen is a city of five hundred and eighty thousand mortals spread across fifty districts, but the number is misleading. The city dissolves into the Ruhr conurbation without clear borders — cross a street in Altenessen and you are in Gelsenkirchen, drive south from Werden and you are in the Bergisches Land before you notice the transition. The city's identity is not geographic. It is institutional. Essen is the place where the Krupp works were. The rest is suburbs and habit.

The districts that matter to Kindred divide along lines that mortal planning documents do not record.

**The Center** — the pedestrian zone around the Hauptbahnhof, the Limbecker Platz shopping complex, the Rathenaustraße commercial corridor. This is neutral ground in practice if not in protocol. The domain's governing principle in the center is that Kindred who are seen hunting here answer to the Sheriff personally, and the Sheriff's definition of "seen" is broader than most Kindred expect. Feeding in the center is permitted but risky. The Second Inquisition's first Ruhr investigation focused on the municipal administration buildings three blocks from the Hauptbahnhof. The buildings are still there. The investigation's records are not sealed. Be careful.

**The North** — Altenessen, Katernberg, Stoppenberg, Vogelheim. Working-class districts, high immigrant population, persistent unemployment. The mines closed here. The people who stayed did so because they could not afford to leave. This is the Sheriff's territory in both the bureaucratic and the practical sense. Dieter Kranz hunts here, and the Kindred who hunt with him are expected to understand that the mortals in these districts are not prey but people — a distinction the Sheriff enforces with more conviction than protocol requires. The feeding environment is rich in volume but thin in quality, and the Sheriff's displeasure at wasteful hunting is the closest thing to an Anarch sermon you will hear from a domain officer.

**The South** — Bredeney, Werden, Heisingen, the Villa Hügel grounds. Wealthy, green, quiet. The mansions along the Ruhr River were built for Krupp executives who have been dead for a century. Their descendants maintain the gardens and vote Free Democratic. The feeding here is sparse but select — corporate board members, foundation trustees, the elderly inheritors of industrial fortunes that no longer produce anything. The Toreador maintain a presence in Bredeney through cultural board memberships rather than territorial claims. The Ventrue hold no formal territory in the south because they hold something more durable: the foundations that own the land.

**The University Quarter** — around the Universität Duisburg-Essen campus and the Stadtgarten. Thirty-five thousand students, rotating every four years, largely unaware that their campus sits on top of the most contested feeding territory in the domain. The student population provides the domain's only self-renewing herd, and the competition for access is managed through the acknowledgment protocol rather than direct conflict. Magister Brenner's chantry is in this quarter, on the edge of the campus, in a building that the university's facilities department believes is a private research institute. The university has never asked what it researches.

**The Cultural Corridor** — Aalto-Theater, Philharmonie, Museum Folkwang, the Grillo-Theater, connected by a pedestrian axis that Eva Sonnenberg has spent forty years curating into something that feels like Elysium even when it is not. The Keeper's influence here is architectural as much as political — the lighting, the sightlines, the placement of benches and the timing of intervals all serve functions that mortals experience as atmosphere and Kindred experience as infrastructure.

## Transport and Movement

The domain's rail infrastructure is unusually important to Kindred movement. The U-Bahn and S-Bahn networks connect Essen to every major city in the Ruhr, and the underground stations provide transit corridors that Kindred can use without exposure to sunlight. The Hauptbahnhof is a hub, but it is also monitored, and the domain's protocol is that Kindred in transit through the Hauptbahnhof do not hunt, do not linger, and do not attract attention. The Ghost Station — Chapter 17 treats it fully — is the domain's private transit node, accessible through routes that most Kindred learn only after their acknowledgment period.

Surface movement follows the A40 in the north and the B227 in the south. The A40 is perpetually congested, which makes it useless for emergencies and invaluable for anonymity — a Kindred in a car on the A40 at midnight is indistinguishable from a hundred thousand mortals doing the same thing. The domain maintains a small fleet of vehicles registered to institutions that have no obvious connection to Kindred activity. The Sheriff's office will provide access upon request.

## The Feeding Environment

Essen's feeding ecology is managed, not natural. The Seneschal's office maintains a feeding registry that assigns territories and categories to each recognized Kindred, based on clan requirements, domain seniority, and the ecological principle that no territory should be hunted frequently enough to produce a pattern that mortal authorities might notice.

Ventrue feed from the foundation boards, the corporate management tiers, the university administration. The domain's Ventrue population is small — four recognized Kindred — and the feeding niche is adequate to their numbers. The Ventrue who cannot find their preferred vessel in Essen usually relocate to Düsseldorf within a year.

Toreador feed from the cultural institutions, the Philharmonie audience, the theater crowd. The feeding is rich but seasonal — the concert season runs September through June, and the summer months require adaptation or travel. Eva Sonnenberg maintains a personal herd of approximately thirty mortals associated with the cultural sector, but she has never disclosed the nature of the association, and the Toreador who ask are not invited to ask again.

Brujah feed from the north, the working-class districts, the union offices that still exist even though the industries they organized are gone. The feeding is reliable but not luxurious, and the Brujah who complain about this are reminded that the Sheriff hunts in the same districts and does not complain.

Tremere feed from the university and the research institutes. The feeding here is narrow — Magister Brenner's feeding restriction requires vessels with academic credentials — but the university is large enough to sustain it. The Tremere who cannot meet Brenner's restriction have either left the domain or starved. Those are the only two outcomes Brenner's feeding category permits.

Nosferatu feed from the underground, the homeless population, the transit system's marginal spaces. The domain does not track Nosferatu feeding patterns because the Nosferatu do not report them, and no one in the Seneschal's office has ever been able to determine how many Nosferatu are actually in the domain. The official registry lists two. The Ghost Station's occupancy suggests more.

## The Seasons

Essen has seasons, and they matter. Winter is long, dark, and wet — sixteen hours of darkness in December, which makes it the domain's active season. Summer is short, bright, and disorienting — June nights last barely six hours, and the Kindred who have not made arrangements for feeding and shelter during the compressed darkness either adapt or leave. The domain's summer population is notably smaller than its winter population. No one acknowledges this publicly, but the feeding registry records it, and I note it here because you should know that the Kindred you meet in July are the ones who have committed to this city in ways that the winter visitors have not.

## What You Should Know About the People

The mortals of Essen are not friendly in the way that Cologne is friendly or Munich is friendly. They are closed, reserved, slow to trust, and slower to forgive. The *Ruhrpott* identity is not an affectation — it is the accumulated residue of a century of industrial labour, fifty years of structural decline, and the particular pride of people who know that the rest of Germany considers their region provincial and uninteresting and have decided to agree with that assessment rather than argue.

You will not charm them quickly. You will not seduce them with displays of wealth or culture — they have seen wealth disappear and culture arrive as a replacement for employment, and they distrust both. What works is constancy. Show up. Be present. Do not promise anything you cannot deliver. The mortals who accept you will do so slowly, and their acceptance, once given, is a resource the domain's institutions have been built on for a hundred and sixty-five years.

Do not waste it.

— K.W.
