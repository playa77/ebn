# Chapter 8: The Anarchs: A Narrative Without a Homeland

> *"They're not wrong. The city is dying. They're just wrong about whose fault it is and what you do about it."*
> — Dieter Kranz, Sheriff of Essen, to a deputy, 2020

---

*The Seneschal asked me to write this chapter. She said I was the only domain officer the Anarchs would trust to be fair. I don't know if that's a compliment to me or an indictment of the rest of the court. I do know that I've been walking these streets since my Embrace in 1962, and I've known every Anarch in Essen since the movement first arrived here in the 1990s, and I haven't killed any of them. That's more than most Sheriffs can say.*

*Here's the truth about the Anarchs in Essen: they're about a dozen Kindred with a political argument that gets stronger every year the city declines. They're not a threat to the domain. They're a threat to the domain's story about itself. The difference matters.*

---

## The Narrative

The Anarch argument in Essen is simple and, from certain angles, persuasive. The Camarilla has governed this city for a hundred and sixty-five years. In that time, the mortal city has experienced two world wars, deindustrialization, mass unemployment, and the slow hollowing-out of the working-class communities that gave the Ruhr its identity. The Camarilla preserved itself. It did not preserve the city.

The Anarchs do not propose to fix Essen. They propose that the Camarilla cannot fix Essen, because the Camarilla's institutional architecture was designed to maintain Kindred stability, not mortal prosperity, and the two goals have diverged. Friedrich built a machine for governing vampires. He did not build a machine for governing people. The people noticed.

This argument has gained traction since Vienna. The old Camarilla — the Camarilla of Justicars and Archons and the credible threat of elder intervention — could dismiss Anarch critiques as the noise of irrelevant youth. The post-Vienna Camarilla cannot dismiss anything. Its authority is thinner than it has been in centuries, and the Anarchs in Essen know it.

The movement's informal leader is Rika Bohm, a Brujah Embraced in 1995, a former labor organizer whose sire was killed in 2004 by a mortal hunter who was never identified and whom Bohm has never stopped believing was directed by a Camarilla elder. She is intelligent, charismatic, and entirely convinced that the Camarilla is an obstacle to everything the Ruhr needs to survive. She is also, in my assessment, insufficiently curious about what the Nosferatu are guarding, and that lack of curiosity will eventually become a problem.

---

## Three Factions

The Anarchs in Essen are not unified. They are held together by a shared critique of the Camarilla and by nothing else.

**The Pragmatists** — about six Kindred, led by Bohm — believe the Anarchs should operate within the domain's existing structure, building influence in the municipal administration and the cultural sector, demonstrating that Anarch governance is more effective than Camarilla governance by actually governing something. They maintain feeding territories in the northern districts, observe acknowledgment protocols (grudgingly), and attend Elysium when required (rarely). The domain tolerates them because they are not attempting to destabilize anything. The domain is wrong about what toleration looks like from the Anarch side. They are not waiting. They are preparing.

**The Revisionists** — three Kindred, including a Toreador defector named Lisel Mueller who left Eva's circle in 2018 — believe the Anarchs should challenge the Camarilla's historical narrative directly, exposing the domain's secrets, forcing the Council to account for what it has done and what it has allowed to happen. Mueller has been researching the 1847 mining disaster and the domain's subsequent cover-up. She has not found evidence of a cover-up, but she has found evidence that the official record is incomplete. The difference matters more to her than it does to me.

**The Underground Faction** — two Nosferatu who do not identify themselves to me or to anyone in the formal Anarch movement — operates in the tunnels, maintains contact with the wardens, and appears to believe that the Nosferatu's knowledge of what lies beneath the city gives the Anarchs leverage over the Camarilla that the Pragmatists and Revisionists have not yet recognized. I have attempted to communicate with this faction through intermediaries. The intermediaries do not return. I do not know whether this means the Underground Faction is hostile or simply uninterested. Either possibility concerns me.

---

## The Nosferatu Problem

The Anarchs' relationship with the Nosferatu is the most complex political dynamic in the domain, and the one that would most benefit from a conversation that no one is willing to have.

The Anarchs rely on the Nosferatu for access to the underground network — the tunnels and stations and service corridors that allow movement through the city without surface exposure. The Nosferatu grant this access, selectively, because the Anarchs are useful to them: a political movement on the surface that keeps the Camarilla's attention focused upward, away from the underground, is a political movement the wardens want to maintain. The Nosferatu do not care about Anarch ideology. They care about Anarch distraction.

The Anarchs do not understand this. They believe the Nosferatu share their opposition to the Camarilla on principle. They do not. The Nosferatu have been in Essen since before the Camarilla existed. They do not oppose the Camarilla. They tolerate it, because the Camarilla's institutions keep the surface stable and the surface's stability keeps the underground sealed. The Anarchs' proposal for Zollverein independence — treated fully in Chapter 30 — would threaten that seal. The Nosferatu will not allow it.

The Anarchs do not know this. When they learn it, the movement will fracture. I am not certain which side I will be on.

> ## STORYTELLER ONLY — THE HIDDEN LEVERAGE
>
> The Anarchs believe their primary asset is their moral argument. They are wrong. Their primary asset is the Nosferatu's tolerance, and that tolerance is conditional on the Anarchs not doing anything that threatens the seals.
>
> The Nosferatu have been watching the Anarch movement since its inception. They have agents in all three factions. They know what Bohm is planning before Bohm knows. They have allowed the movement to exist because a visible, noisy opposition on the surface makes the surface easier to manage — the Camarilla expends energy containing the Anarchs that it would otherwise expend looking underground.
>
> If the Anarchs' Zollverein proposal advances to the point of threatening Shaft 12, the Nosferatu will intervene — not politically, not diplomatically, but directly. The wardens have maintained the seals for seven hundred years. They will not allow a political movement to compromise what they have spent seven centuries protecting.
>
> The tragedy of the Anarchs in Essen is that they are fighting for a city worth saving, and the city is built on top of something the wardens will not allow them to touch. The Anarchs do not know they are fighting on a minefield. The minefield is older than the fight.

## What the Anarchs Offer

If you are creating a character aligned with the Anarch movement, here is what the movement can give you and what it cannot.

The Anarchs can offer community — a dozen Kindred who know each other, watch each other's backs, and share information the Camarilla does not have. They can offer purpose — a reason to be in Essen that is not "the Prince permitted it." They can offer access to the underground through their Nosferatu contacts, though the access is conditional and the conditions are not disclosed in advance.

The Anarchs cannot offer protection. The domain does not persecute them, but it does not protect them either. An Anarch who violates protocol will face the Sheriff's office the same as any other Kindred. An Anarch who threatens the seals will face something worse than the Sheriff's office, and the domain will not intervene.

The Anarchs cannot offer victory. The Camarilla is not going to fall in Essen. The institutions are too deeply embedded, the Prince's authority is too structurally reinforced, the Nosferatu's interests are too aligned with stability. What the Anarchs can offer is the argument — the persistent, necessary argument that the domain's stability has costs, and that those costs are borne by Kindred who did not consent to bear them.

I am not an Anarch. I am the Sheriff who enforces the domain they oppose. But I have been walking these streets since before the movement arrived, and I will be walking them after it leaves, and I will not pretend the argument is without merit. What I will say is this: the argument is not the whole story, and the part of the story the Anarchs do not know is the part that would destroy them if they learned it.

Be careful what you fight for. The ground you are standing on is not what you think it is.

— D.K.
